The Meaning of Life According to Different Philosophical Currents

Since forever, humanity has wondered about the purpose of its existence. Philosophy has addressed this question from different currents, offering visions that remain relevant today.

Existentialism (Sartre, Kierkegaard): There is no predetermined meaning; each person must construct their own through their decisions. This entails freedom but also responsibility and anguish.

Nihilism (Nietzsche): Denies the existence of an absolute purpose. With the "death of God," traditional values are lost, but the possibility opens to create new ones through the ideal of the overman.

Modern Humanism: Proposes that meaning is built collectively through knowledge, ethics, and social cooperation.

Conclusion:
There is no single answer to the meaning of life. Philosophy invites us to question ourselves, reflect, and seek meaning, either individually or collectively.

Ethics and Technology in the 21st Century

The development of technologies such as artificial intelligence, biotechnology, and automation has generated ethical dilemmas that require philosophical reflection.

Kant: His categorical imperative demands that our actions could become universal laws. Applied to technology, this means AI must respect human dignity.

Utilitarianism (Bentham and Mill): Justifies technologies if they generate the greatest possible wellbeing for the majority, such as medical algorithms or genetic editing.

Political Philosophy and Democracy in Crisis

Nowadays, democracy faces challenges such as polarization, misinformation, and loss of trust in institutions. Political philosophy provides key ideas to analyze and respond to this crisis.

Platon: Proposed that rulers should be philosophers, emphasizing the importance of wise and ethical leaders.

Rousseau: Defended the social contract, where power arises from the agreement among citizens. This relates today to citizen participation and transparency.

Hannah Arendt: Warned about the dangers of political lies and mass manipulation, highly relevant in the era of fake news.

Current crisis: Social media intensifies polarization and spreads misinformation, weakening public debate and democratic quality.

Conclusion:
Political philosophy is essential to renew democracy, strengthening institutions that are fairer, more transparent, and resistant to manipulation.
